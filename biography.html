The Biography

First years: obscure beginnings

Sant'Anna di Palazzo Church

Giovan Battista Basile was born on 3rd February 1583 in a middle-class family from Naples. The exact location of his birth has been object of constant debate since scholars started to seriously focus on this peculiar figure in neapolitan history. According to some studies, his birthplace is to be found in Giugliano in Campania, a town in the modern day province of Naples. This hypothesis was first proposed by Andrea Tagliatela, who discovered a baptismal certificate in the city with the name of of the author and the year 1566 as his birth year; this thesis was then reproposed later by Emanuele Coppola in his 1998 essay Giovan Battista Basile nacque a Giugliano nel 1566, and it quickly became common knowledge. In 2023 though, a small revolution in the studies on Basile took place: Domenico Antonio D'Alessandro, in his essay Giovan Battista Basile tra "favole" campanilistiche e realtà documentaria revealed that the writer was born in 1583 from Francesco Antonio Basile and Cornelia Daniele, as the certifacte of the baptism describes:

A dì 4 de febraro 1583, io detto don Lorenzo de Feo ho baptizato Giovan Baptista Biasi, ﬁglio [de] lo signor Francesco Antonio Basile et de la signora Cornelia Daniele ad Monte Calvario, la comare la signora Agata Cicala, la mammana Rosa De Santo, per clerico Vicenso Francese.

This theory seems to be confirmed by two details that come out from the sources and the biography of Basile:

  1. His sister Adriana Basile's baptism certificate was redacted in the same church three years later; it's possible that the family lived in the area.
  2. In his seminal work Lo cunto de Li Cunti, Basile never makes any references to Giugliano. This is a peculiar thing for an author that consistently uses references to locations where he actually lived and spent time.

As it was required for a person of his social class, Basile grew up in the complex system of parrish education in Naples. It's here that he met and became life friend with another incredible neapolitan author, Giulio Cesare Cortese, the author of the "Vaiasseide". Their friendship seemingly lasted for the entirety of their lives, and a testimony of this is the reccurent amount of references between the two in their literary works. Cortese directly makes a reference to his friend in his work The Travel to Parnaso:

Che la fortuna ammico me facette / Da che ghieva a la scola peccerillo

Another important testimony of their relationship is the big correspondence they held all throught their incredibly messy and complex lives, constantly quoted and references by both in their works.
